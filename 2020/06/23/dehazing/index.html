Hello Hexo

Recently I had the spare time to write on the blog again for the first time in 2 years, as well as to renovate the look and feel of the site.

The major difference is that the blog, rather than using Jekyll, now uses Hexo. To celebrate, I decided to wrap an old project I did a few years ago in a nicer GUI.

How to use the image dehazer

The image dehazer can be found here. You can find the source code over at Github. To use it,

  1. Pick the photo you want to dehaze by clicking “Browse”. It is worth noting that the dehazer works best on unedited, non HDR/tone mapped photos.

  2. Some options will now be present:

  • The Gamma value of the input photo. In most digital cameras, photos are generally processed such that the colours are not linear to the amount of light a pixel receives to match light perception of the human eye. Change this value to restore this linearity. Typically this value should be between 0.8 and 2.0.
  • The Minimum and maximum brightness of the air light. In dehazing models that don't require deep learning, the atmosphere color (a.k.a. the air light) in the photo is gnerally considered to be one constant color. Here, the air light is automatically calculated, but the user must input the minimum and maximum brightness of the air light. In photos that are more gloomy, the air light can be deceptively dark, so if no value works for the photo, try setting a lower value for the minimum air light.
  1. Press “Dehaze!”. The program should run for a few seconds before calculating the final image. The estimated air light should be shown after “Air light estimate”, followed by two images:
  • On the left is the dehazed image.
  • On the right is the calculated transmittance map for the image. The darker the pixel, the more haze is present.
  1. Finally, several options can be used to further post process the image:
  • Distance uniformly adds or subtracts the amount of haze in the entire photo.
  • Haze amount controls how much the photo is dehazed. A value of 0 results in the photo being completely dehazed, while a value of 1 returns the original photo. You could also add the amount of haze in the photo by assigning a value higher than 1.
  • Gamma adjusts the gamma of the photo once more. This value should typically be identical to the previous gamma input, though you can adjust this to your liking.
  1. All of the operations above are calculated on a photo of reduced size. If you're feeling extra adventurous, you can also click “Calculate original size” to dehaze the original photo, though this will take several minutes to compute. Once the dehazing is finished, you can click on “Download” to download the photo.

Since this website is generated on a static site generator, all operations are performed on the front end. This means that everything is calculated locally on your computer, no images are uploaded, and I'm unable to steal your photos to collect private data/train neural networks/report to the CIA, etc. Please do try to use a better computer, and I would strongly recommend against running the web app on your phone.
